Dataset

The precipitation dataset comes from the Colombian open data portal (datos.gov.co) and contains rainfall measurements from weather stations spread across the country. Each record captures a single observation from a physical station along with its geographic coordinates, timestamp, and rainfall amount in millimetres.

Preprocessing

The raw CSV uses an unusual double-quote wrapping format and stores numeric values with Spanish locale decimal separators (commas instead of dots). The preprocessing script applied the following steps before any modelling could begin.

  • Strip outer double-quotes from every row and unescape inner quotes.
  • Convert ValorObservado, Latitud and Longitud from comma-decimal to dot-decimal format.
  • Parse FechaObservacion timestamps into proper datetime objects.
  • Extract hour, month and day_of_week as additional time features.
  • Normalise text columns (strip whitespace, uppercase) for consistent grouping.
  • Aggregate per station to produce a 33-row feature matrix — one row per physical station.

Advanced K-Means Clustering

The clustering pipeline was substantially improved to achieve a more robust and machine-learning-oriented grouping of the 33 weather stations. The final model uses an advanced K-Means approach combined with feature engineering, reliability weighting, robust scaling, dimensionality reduction using PCA, and multiple validation metrics.

Instead of using only basic precipitation statistics, the new pipeline incorporates temporal, statistical, and spatial characteristics that allow the algorithm to better capture hidden precipitation patterns among stations. A total of 4 clusters were selected to obtain more meaningful and interpretable environmental groupings.

  • Advanced features used: mean, median, variance, standard deviation, precipitation range, interquartile range (IQR), skewness, rainy day ratio, latitude, longitude, temporal cyclic variables, seasonal behaviour, coefficient of variation, and observation reliability.
  • Reliability weighting: stations with more observations receive higher influence during training, improving cluster stability and reducing noise from sparse stations.
  • Scaling: RobustScaler was applied instead of StandardScaler to reduce the impact of extreme precipitation outliers.
  • PCA dimensionality reduction: Principal Component Analysis (PCA) preserved 95% of the explained variance while reducing noise and redundant correlations between features.
  • Clustering algorithm: MiniBatchKMeans was used for better computational efficiency and scalability while maintaining clustering quality.
  • Final number of clusters: 4 clusters were selected to achieve more detailed environmental segmentation and improved interpretability.
  • Validation metrics: silhouette score, Davies–Bouldin index, and Calinski–Harabasz score were used to evaluate clustering quality and separation.
  • Random state: 42, ensuring reproducibility of the clustering process.
